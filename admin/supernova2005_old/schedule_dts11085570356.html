Is Government the Problem or the Solution?
Friday, June 25, 2004, 2:30pm - 3:30pm

Policy developments in areas such as broadband deployment, content regulation, intellectual property, Internet taxation, telecom regulation, privacy, and outsourcing have significant impacts on innovation. The tech industry complains that it just wants government off its back… except when it wants help, subsidies, or other changes in the law. Beyond the usual political rhetoric, what is the right way to think about technology policy? How will the business and technical trend toward decentralization affect both the participants and the substance of these debates?


Scott Blake Harris, Managing Partner, Harris Wiltshire & Grannis

Scott Blake Harris is Managing Partner of Harris, Wiltshire & Grannis LLP, a boutique law firm with telecommunications, litigation, and appellate practices. From 1994 to 1996, Scott was the chief of the FCC’s International Bureau. Since leaving the FCC, he has been a member of the Advisory Board of Virginia Tech’s Center for Wireless Communications, Chairman of the FCC’s Advisory Committee for WRC-97, Co-Chair of the Council on Foreign Relations Study Group on Information Technology and Foreign Policy, Co-Chair of the FCBA’s International and Online Practice Committees, and an Adjunct Professor at Georgetown Law Center. Scott also served as Chief Counsel for Export Administration at the Commerce Department. Scott is a magna cum laude graduate of both Brown University and Harvard Law School.


Peter Harter, Managing Principal, Farrington Group

A leading Internet law and public policy authority, Peter Harter advises individuals, companies and non-profits on relationship capital with a focus on investor and customer development. Previously, as Securify’s SVP for Business Development and Public Policy, Peter created policy and revenue relationships with senior government officials and industry executives. He was elected by peers to two consecutive terms as Chair of the Information Security Committee of the Information Technology Association of America. Prior to Securify, Peter was VP Global Public Policy & Standards for EMusic, focusing on the Secure Digital Music Initiative. Industry executives elected Peter as President of the Digital Media Association. And at the beginning of his career in Silicon Valley Peter was Global Public Policy Counsel for Netscape. Peter helped shape the Technology Network, the industry’s CEO and VC led political action committee, by serving as the first Chair of the Public Policy Committee.

Since co-founding the Internet Law & Policy Forum in 1995 Peter has been working across industries and countries to build relationships that support an objective approach for the discovery and production of expert positions that gain consensus and benefit the community. Peter holds a B.A. in Rhetoric & Government from Lehigh University and a J.D. from Villanova Law School. In its July 1998 issue, Business 2.0 named him one of "The 25 Most Intriguing Minds of the New Economy." Peter began using the Internet in 1986.


Chris Marsden, Oxford Internet Institute

Christopher Marsden joined the Internet Institute on 1 May 2004. His research focuses on cyberlaw and international political economy/Internet governance, broadband mobile Internet regulation and Internet video policy. He edited "Convergence in European Digital TV Regulation" (1999) and "Regulating the Global Information Society" (2000). He is an Editorial Board Member of the journal ‘info’ (2003-date) and the Phoenix Center for Advanced Law and Public Policy Studies (2001-date). Previously he was a fellow at Warwick Business School (2001-2), the Globalisation Centre (2000-1), the Harvard Information Infrastructure Project (1999-2000) and was a founding co-editor of www.ijclp.org He was the project manager of the IAPCODE project www.selfregulation.info.
