The ethic of whirrled is captured perfectly by the science that Nobel Laureate Murry Gell-Mann has termed "plectics" -- the study of the simple and the complex. Plectics encompasses disciplines such as chaos theory and complexity, but also emphasizes the quest for simplicity amid seeming randomness. Doyne Farmer, one of the leading developers of chaos theory, makes the point explicitly. There are two sides of the branch of mathmatics known as chaos theory. One side, the "Jurassic Park" branch, is about finding daunting complexity in things that we think are easily understood and controlled. Complexity theory demonstrates that for sufficiently complex systems, such as an ecosystem or the New York Stock Exchange, tiny variations in inputs can result in vastly different outputs. No matter how well you understand the original conditions of a system, you cannot reliably predict where the system will go in the future, because its development is contingent on so many inter-related variables and feedback loops. The flip side of complexity theory, however, is that in seeming randomness there may be pockets of order. Fractal geometry allows extremely realistic scenes of nature to be drawn from stunningly simple mathmatical formulas. Even more significantly, order will inevitably emerge from a system that is sufficiently complex. This order arises not from any conscious design, but from the emergent properties of independent and interdependent elements. Biologist Stuart Kauffman calls this "order for free." He makes the argument that "complex adaptive systems" self-organize not as the result of pure chance, but as the result of inherent properties of complexity. So what does all this have to do with whirrled? A great deal.
